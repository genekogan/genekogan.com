<p>
	<i>Listening to the Ocean on a Shore of Gypsum Sand</i> is a collaboration with <a href="http://www.phillipstearns.com">Phillip Stearns</a> and <a href="http://areyoudevoted.squarespace.com/artists/tag/dan-tesene">Dan Tesene</a> which investigates the sonic properties of real and imagined seashells. The seashells are algorithmically designed in custom software written in Processing and fabricated by printing and binding thin layers of gypsum powder.
</p>
<p>
	In <a href="http://phillipstearns.wordpress.com/2012/08/24/3d-printed-seashells/">Phil's words</a>, the "project questions the role of experience in the mediation of the virtual world to the real world and visa versa." The software allows the generation of both real shells found in the seas, and ones totally unfound in nature (that we know of). How does the experience of listening for the ocean in these virtual shells influence our perception of real shells and how is this interaction changing in a world with technologically mediated experiences increasingly crossing over into the real.
</p>
	
<p>
	The process started with a software program I wrote in Processing, which models the formation of Mollusk seashells and generates printable meshes, based <a href="http://www.mat.uc.pt/~picado/conchas/eng/article.pdf">on a paper by Jorge Picado</a>. The original design program and usage instructions are <a href="http://www.github.com/genekogan/SeashellGenerator">downloadable via Github</a>. Custom modifications deforming the surface and orientation of the shells were added by Phil who then designed the shells seen below. Dan then fabricated the meshes by printing and binding thin layers of gypsum powder.
</p>
	
<p>
	The project was on display in September at <a href="http://soundwalk.org/artists/phillip-stearns-dan-tesene-gene-kogan/">Soundwalk 2012</a>, an annual sound art exhibition in Long Beach, Los Angeles, CA. Below are some of the shells we created; a full gallery can be found <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/phillipstearns/sets/72157631299114642/">here</a>.
</p>
