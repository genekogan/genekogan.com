Listening to the Ocean on a Shore of Gypsum Sand is a collaboration with Phillip Stearns and Dan Tesene which investigates the sonic properties of real and imagined seashells. The seashells are algorithmically designed in custom software written in Processing and fabricated by printing and binding thin layers of gypsum powder.

In Phil's words, the "project questions the role of experience in the mediation of the virtual world to the real world and visa versa." The software allows the generation of both real shells found in the seas, and ones totally undiscovered in nature. How does the experience of listening for the ocean in these virtual shells influence our perception of real shells and how is this interaction changing in a world with technologically mediated experiences increasingly crossing over into the real.

The design process is carried out in a Processing program which models the formation of Mollusc seashells and generates printable meshes. The mathematical formulation is based on a paper by Jorge Picado and incorporates a number of custom modifications deforming the surface and orientation of the shells. The original design program and usage instructions are hosted on GitHub. The designs exported into meshes by the software are then fabricated by printing and binding thin layers of gypsum powder.
